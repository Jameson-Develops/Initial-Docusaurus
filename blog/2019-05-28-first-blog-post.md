I've always been inherently good at most things. 

When I was young, my father left our family. My brothers moved out shortly thereafter and I was left man of the house at the ripe old age of 16. I learned early on that for my mother, failure wasn't an option. 

My mother Bev had always resorted to physical and emotional abuse to 'help' me behave, but it only got worse. No, I'm not referring to spanking as physical abuse. To give you some insight; when I was 14 (before dad left), my mother's spies at my school informed her that I had kissed a girl. When I returned home that day, she screamed at me for roughly 30 minutes as she outlined how I had failed in every way possible including but not limited to ruining my eternal salvation. When she had worked herself up to the point of not knowing what to do with the extreme emotion she was feeling, my mother balled up her right hand into a tight fist and, in the blink of an eye, pulled her elbow up as she suckerpunched me. Bev walloped her 14 year old son square in the face. I was leaning against the back of our red couch when that torpedo of a fist flew at me. I remember it vividly, because as I stood there arms crossed gently across my body, she hit me with such force that I rolled over the back of that couch and continued rolling until I was face down on the old tightly-woven area rug between our red couch and bubble tv sitting in that old wood grain tv stand. 

When I stood up, my eyes brimmed with tears. Not only was I just squarely socked in the face by a woman who grew up on a farm (that matters because she was not weak), my mother and what I thought was supposed to be my safe place had once again shown me otherwise. This was the result of failing Bev and as the punsihments got worse, I learned that if I didn't fail or wasn't caught failing, I could spare myself a beating.

I learned everything wrong about failure.

See, the thing that Bev didn't understand was how to teach her growing child how to build a healthy relationship with anyone or anything let alone how to build one with failure. Now that I'm working on a homelab of all things, I'm realizing how skewed my view of failure has been. Failure isn't something to fear. Fear is paralyzing. Failure is a tool to urge you on to something greater! Failure is an outcome of trying, and so often people try then fail then quit. One of my favorites: When asked about his progress with lightbulbs and his failures with the same, Thomas Edison would tell reporters he hasn't failed, he's learned "1,000 ways not to make a light bulb." The entire process is meant to further learning, not to reach the goal. If everything were done perfectly the first try, we'd be much farther along as a society. 

Failure isn't an endpoint. It's a vital and valuable part of any process. I'm learning the true value of failure and I encourage you to do the same.
